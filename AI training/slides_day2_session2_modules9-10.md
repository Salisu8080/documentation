## Slide 2: The AI Use Spectrum — Where Is the Line?

| Generally Acceptable | Gray Area | Generally Prohibited |
|---------------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Brainstorming ideas | Having AI write substantial drafts you edit | Submitting AI text as your own scholarship |
| Grammar/style editing | AI-generated assessment without adaptation | Fabricating data or results |
| Generating examples for teaching | Heavy reliance on AI for research direction | Undisclosed AI use in publications |
| Outlining and structuring | Using AI to interpret your data | Using AI to complete student assignments |
| Summarizing your own work | Paraphrasing to avoid plagiarism detection | Bypassing peer review with AI |

**The guiding principle: Transparency**
- Disclose how you used AI and to what extent
- Most journals now require an "AI Use Statement"
- Course syllabi should state your AI policy for students

## Slide 4: Data Privacy — What Never to Put Into AI Tools

**When you type into a public AI tool, your data may be:**
- Stored on company servers
- Used to train future models (some tools, some settings)
- Accessible to company employees for quality review

**Never input into public AI tools:**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Student data** | Names, matric numbers, grades, personal info |
| **Confidential research** | Unpublished findings, raw data with identifiers, interview transcripts |
| **Institutional info** | Exam papers before administration, personnel records, budgets |
| **Peer review content** | Manuscripts under confidential review |

**Safe practice:** Anonymize all data before input. Use institutional AI tools when available.

## Slide 6: Advanced Technique — Prompt Chaining

**For complex tasks: break into sequential prompts where each builds on the previous**

**Why it works better than one massive prompt:**
- Each step is focused → AI performs better
- You can review and correct at each step
- Reduces hallucination risk
- More control over the final output

**The basic pattern:**
```
Step 1: Outline → Review →
Step 2: Draft each part → Review →
Step 3: Refine → Review →
Step 4: Polish/Finalize
```

**Example — Research Paper Discussion Section:**
1. "Outline my Discussion section with 5 key points based on my TAM study findings"
2. "Develop Point 1: my finding that perceived usefulness was strongest predictor (beta=0.42). How does this compare to existing literature?"
3. "Review my draft paragraph — identify logical gaps and weak connections to theory" [paste your draft]
4. "Edit for academic clarity and flow, maintaining my voice" [paste revised paragraph]

> Speaker notes: 5 minutes on the concept. Emphasize the "review at each step" principle — that is what makes chaining powerful. Full prompt chaining strategies with academic examples in **Module 10, Lesson 1**.

## Slide 7: Multi-Tool Workflows

**No single AI tool excels at everything. Use the right tool for each sub-task:**

| Stage | Best Tool | Why |
|-------|-----------|-----|
| **Paper discovery** | Elicit, Perplexity, Consensus | Built for research, provides sources |
| **Long document analysis** | Claude | 200K token context window |
| **Writing & brainstorming** | ChatGPT, Claude | Creative, instruction-following |
| **Fact-checking** | Perplexity, Google Scholar | Provides sourced answers |
| **Grammar & polish** | Grammarly, Quillbot | Specialized editing tools |

**Practical multi-tool workflow:**
1. Discover papers (Elicit/Perplexity) →
2. Analyze themes across papers (Claude) →
3. Draft sections (ChatGPT) →
4. Verify facts (Perplexity/Google Scholar) →
5. Polish writing (Grammarly)

> Speaker notes: 3 minutes. Key insight: use research tools for facts and general AI for creative tasks — this reduces hallucination. Full multi-tool strategies in **Module 10, Lesson 2**. Do not over-engineer — one tool is enough for simple tasks.

## Slide 8: Building Your Prompt Template Library

**The most productive AI users do not write prompts from scratch every time.**

**A good template includes:**
1. **Template name** — what task it is for
2. **The prompt** — with clear [PLACEHOLDERS]
3. **Placeholder instructions** — what to fill in
4. **Technique used** — which method (5-component, few-shot, chain-of-thought, role-based)
5. **Usage notes** — tips for best results

**Example: Lesson Plan Generator**
> You are an experienced [SUBJECT] professor who uses [TEACHING APPROACH].
> Create a [DURATION]-minute lesson plan on "[TOPIC]" for [STUDENT LEVEL] students who [PRIOR KNOWLEDGE].
> Include: [COMPONENTS]. Format as [STRUCTURE]. Constraints: [LIMITS].

**Store templates in:** A document, spreadsheet, or note-taking app you will actually use.

## Slide 9: Hands-On — Build Your Prompt Toolkit (40 min)

**Final Activity: Create 5 Reusable Prompt Templates**

**Step 1 (5 min):** Identify your 5 most common AI-assisted tasks. Choose a mix:
- Teaching: lesson plans, quizzes, feedback, concept explanations, case studies
- Research: question refinement, literature structure, methodology, abstract drafting
- Writing: manuscript feedback, reviewer responses, cover letters

**Step 2 (20 min):** For each task, create a template using this structure:

> **Template Name:** [Name]
> **Category:** Teaching / Research / Writing
> **When to Use:** [Brief description]
> **Technique:** 5-component / few-shot / chain-of-thought / role-based / chaining
>
> **Prompt:**
> [Your complete prompt with [PLACEHOLDERS]]
>
> **Placeholder Guide:**
> - [PLACEHOLDER 1]: [What to fill in]
> - [PLACEHOLDER 2]: [What to fill in]
>
> **Usage Notes:** [Tips for best results]

**Step 3 (10 min):** Test at least 2 templates with real data from your work. Refine based on results.

**Step 4 (5 min):** Organize all 5 templates in one document with a table of contents.
