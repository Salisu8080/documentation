## Slide 2: AI for Research — The Workflow

**AI can accelerate every stage of early research — with caveats:**

| Research Stage | How AI Helps | Risk Level |
|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| Question formulation | Brainstorms angles, narrows scope | Low |
| Literature discovery | Finds themes and search terms | Medium |
| Paper summarization | Structured summaries of abstracts you provide | Medium |
| Methodology design | Suggests designs, compares approaches | Low-Medium |
| Citation generation | **DO NOT TRUST** — hallucinations are common | **High** |

**The golden rule:** Never cite a source based on AI's description alone. Always read the original paper.

## Slide 3: Hands-On — Research Question Refinement (20 min)

**Activity: From Broad Interest to Specific, Researchable Question**

1. **Start with a broad research interest** from your field (e.g., "AI in accounting education")

2. **Use this prompt:**

> You are a research advisor in [YOUR FIELD]. I am interested in [YOUR BROAD TOPIC].
>
> Help me develop a specific research question by thinking step-by-step:
> 1. Suggest 3 focused research angles
> 2. For each, propose a specific, researchable question
> 3. Evaluate each against FINER criteria (Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, Relevant)
> 4. Recommend the strongest one and explain why
>
> Context: I am based at a Nigerian university with access to [YOUR DATA SOURCES]. I have a [TIMELINE] and [RESOURCE CONSTRAINTS].

3. **Select your preferred question** and refine further if needed:
> "The question you recommended is close but too broad. Narrow it further by specifying [population/variable/context]."

4. **Test feasibility:** Ask AI "What methodology would this question require?" — if the method seems impractical, refine further.

> Speaker notes: 15 min working, 5 min sharing. This iterative refinement process is the core skill. Full FINER framework explanation and more examples in **Module 6, Lesson 1**.

## Slide 4: Literature Review — What AI Should and Should Not Do

**Use AI for:**
- Suggesting thematic structure for your review
- Summarizing papers YOU provide (paste the abstract)
- Identifying patterns across papers you have read
- Generating search terms for Google Scholar

**Never use AI for:**
- Generating citations (high hallucination risk)
- Writing literature review paragraphs with references
- Claiming a research gap exists (verify yourself)

**Safe prompt pattern:**
> Help me organize my literature review on "[TOPIC]." Suggest 4-5 thematic sections, what each should cover, and how they build toward my research gap. Do NOT generate summaries of specific papers — I will write those myself.

**Also use research-specific tools:** Elicit, Consensus, Semantic Scholar, Perplexity (for sourced answers)

> Speaker notes: This is a critical distinction. AI is excellent for structure/organization but dangerous for citation generation. Walk through the safe prompt pattern. Full strategies including paper summary templates in **Module 6, Lesson 2**.

## Slide 5: Hands-On — Research Methodology Design (15 min)

**Activity: Get a Methodology Recommendation for Your Research Question**

Using the research question you refined earlier:

> You are a senior research methodologist. My research question is: "[YOUR QUESTION]." I plan to use [FRAMEWORK/THEORY] as my theoretical framework.
>
> Think step-by-step:
> 1. Analyze my question — what type of data do I need?
> 2. Suggest 3 possible research designs and evaluate each on: appropriateness, feasibility in my context, and publication potential
> 3. For your recommended design: outline sampling, data collection, and analysis
> 4. Identify 3 methodological limitations and how to mitigate them
>
> Constraints: I can access [YOUR POPULATION] at [YOUR LOCATION], have a [TIMELINE], and [RESOURCE LIMITS].

**Evaluate the output:**
- Does the recommended design match your question?
- Are the constraints realistic for your context?
- What would you change based on your domain expertise?

## Slide 6: AI for Academic Writing — Key Principles

**AI as writing assistant, not author:**

| Acceptable | Ethically Gray | Prohibited |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| Brainstorming outlines | Having AI write substantial drafts you edit | Submitting AI text as your own scholarship |
| Grammar/clarity editing | AI-generated analysis of findings | Fabricating data with AI |
| Improving flow of YOUR text | Heavy reliance on AI for arguments | Undisclosed substantial AI use |
| Suggesting structure | AI-rewritten methodology sections | Using AI to bypass peer review |

**The workflow:** You draft → AI reviews/suggests → You revise → AI polishes → You finalize

**Always disclose AI use** as required by your institution or target journal.

## Slide 7: Hands-On — Abstract Drafting & Peer Review Response (20 min)

**Activity Part A: Draft an Abstract (10 min)**

> You are an academic writing specialist. Help me draft a structured abstract (max 250 words) for my research on "[YOUR TOPIC]."
>
> Here are my study details:
> - Topic: [TOPIC]
> - Methodology: [DESIGN, SAMPLE SIZE, FRAMEWORK]
> - Key findings: [2-3 MAIN RESULTS]
> - Contribution: [WHAT IS NEW]
>
> Structure: Background (2 sentences), Purpose (1 sentence), Methods (2 sentences), Findings (2-3 sentences), Conclusion (2 sentences). Use formal academic language.

**Activity Part B: Respond to a Reviewer Comment (10 min)**

Respond to this simulated reviewer comment:
> "The methodology section lacks sufficient detail about sampling procedures and data analysis techniques. Please clarify how participants were selected and provide more information about instrument validity."

Draft a point-by-point response that acknowledges the concern, describes changes, and maintains a professional tone. Use your own research context.

## Slide 8: Critical Evaluation — Why This Matters Most

**AI hallucinations in academic work can destroy credibility:**

| Hallucination Type | Example | Risk |
|-------------------|---------|------|
| Fabricated citations | AI invents author names, paper titles, journals | **Critical** |
| Misattributed findings | Real paper, wrong description of what it found | **High** |
| Invented statistics | "Studies show 73% of..." with no actual source | **High** |
| False historical claims | Confident but incorrect dates, events, quotes | **Medium** |

**Why it happens:** AI predicts text that *looks like* a citation/statistic — it does not verify anything.

**The confidence problem:** AI presents fabricated content with the same certainty as accurate content. There is no visible difference.

> Speaker notes: This is the most important awareness slide of the entire training. Give 3-4 minutes here. Full explanation of why hallucinations occur in **Module 8, Lesson 1**. Verification strategies in **Module 8, Lesson 2**.

## Slide 9: The Fact-Checking Workflow

**For any AI content you plan to use in academic work:**

**Step 1: Identify what needs checking**
- Citations, statistics, dates, quotes, policy claims

**Step 2: Verify with authoritative sources**
- Citations → Google Scholar, DOI lookup, your library
- Statistics → Trace to the original report
- Facts → Cross-reference with 2+ independent sources

**Step 3: Replace or remove unverified content**

**Red flags that suggest hallucination:**
- Overly specific unsourced numbers ("73.4% of universities...")
- Perfect-sounding citations you have never encountered in your field
- Suspiciously convenient evidence that supports every point perfectly
- Very recent dates (2025-2026) with very specific claims

## Slide 10: Hands-On — Hallucination Detective (25 min)

**Activity: Deliberately Test AI for Hallucinations in Your Field**

**Step 1 (5 min):** Ask AI for a literature review paragraph WITH citations on a topic you know well:

> Provide a 5-sentence literature review paragraph on "[YOUR EXPERT TOPIC]" that includes at least 4 specific citations (author names, years). Discuss key findings and how they relate to each other.

**Step 2 (10 min):** Verify every citation:
- Search each on Google Scholar — does the paper exist?
- If yes, does it say what the AI claims? (Read the abstract)
- If no, mark it as hallucinated

**Step 3 (5 min):** Check factual claims:
- Identify 3+ specific claims in the paragraph
- Verify against independent sources

**Step 4:** Score the output:
- Accuracy (1-5) | Relevance (1-5) | Completeness (1-5)

**Discussion (5 min):** How many citations were real? Were you surprised?

> Speaker notes: This is the most eye-opening activity in the training. Most participants will find that 30-70% of AI-generated citations are fabricated. This experience permanently changes how they interact with AI. Full activity in **Module 8, Lesson 4**.

## Slide 11: Hands-On — Complete Research Workflow Simulation (20 min)

**Activity: Bring It All Together**

Starting from your refined research question, use AI to:

1. **Generate a literature review outline** (structure only, no citations):
> Suggest 4-5 thematic sections for a literature review on "[YOUR QUESTION]." What should each cover? How do they connect? Where is the gap? Do NOT cite specific papers.

2. **Get a methodology recommendation** (if you have not already):
> Given my question and constraints, recommend a research design with sampling, data collection, and analysis plan.

3. **Draft an abstract** for the proposed study (as if it were completed):
> Draft a 250-word abstract for this proposed study: [QUESTION, METHOD, EXPECTED CONTRIBUTION]

4. **Compile into a 1-page research plan summary**

**Critically evaluate:** Where was AI most helpful? Where did you need your own expertise?
