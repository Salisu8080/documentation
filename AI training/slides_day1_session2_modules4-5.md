## Slide 2: The Teaching Materials Prompt Checklist

**When prompting for any teaching material, always specify:**

| Element | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| Subject and specific topic | "Depreciation methods" not "accounting" |
| Student level and prior knowledge | Determines depth and language |
| Duration / length | Prevents too much or too little content |
| Learning objectives | Ensures purposeful content |
| Teaching approach | Lecture, active learning, case-based? |
| Assessment component | How to check understanding |
| Practical constraints | Class size, technology, room setup |

**Template for lesson planning:**
> You are [ROLE]. Create a [DURATION]-minute lesson plan on "[TOPIC]" for [LEVEL] students who [PRIOR KNOWLEDGE]. Include: [COMPONENTS]. Constraints: [LIMITS]. Format as [STRUCTURE].

## Slide 3: Hands-On — Create a Lesson Plan (15 min)

**Activity: Generate a Complete Lesson Plan for Your Topic**

1. **Choose a specific topic** you currently teach or will teach soon
2. **Fill in the template** and submit to your AI tool:

> You are an experienced [YOUR FIELD] professor who uses active learning strategies. Create a [DURATION]-minute lesson plan on "[YOUR TOPIC]" for [STUDENT LEVEL] students who [PRIOR KNOWLEDGE].
>
> Include:
> - 3 learning objectives aligned with Bloom's taxonomy (at least one at application level)
> - An engaging 10-minute opener
> - Main content in timed segments
> - One group activity suitable for [CLASS SIZE] students
> - A 5-minute formative assessment
> - Materials/resources needed
>
> Format as a table with columns: Time, Activity, Description, Learning Objective.
> Constraints: [YOUR REAL CONSTRAINTS — e.g., no laptops, 80 students]

3. **Evaluate the output:** Is it accurate? Feasible? Would you use it?
4. **Refine with a follow-up prompt** if needed (e.g., "Make the group activity simpler" or "Add a Nigerian business example")

> Speaker notes: 12 min working, 3 min quick sharing. Emphasize that the first output is a draft — refinement is normal and expected. Full guidance on iterative refinement in **Module 4, Lesson 1**.

## Slide 4: Assessment Design — Key Principles

**When generating assessments with AI, always specify:**

- **Question types** (MCQ, short-answer, essay, case-based)
- **Difficulty distribution** (e.g., 40% recall, 40% application, 20% analysis)
- **Bloom's taxonomy levels** explicitly
- **Answer keys + explanations** (saves grading time)
- **Topic coverage** (which specific concepts to test)

**Always quality-check AI-generated assessments for:**
- Accuracy of correct answers
- Unambiguous question wording
- Plausible distractors (for MCQs)
- Alignment with your learning objectives

> Speaker notes: Brief overview — 3 minutes. The hands-on follows immediately. Full assessment prompting strategies and examples (MCQs, rubrics, case questions) are in **Module 4, Lesson 2**.

## Slide 5: Hands-On — Generate Assessment Questions (15 min)

**Activity: Create a Quiz Aligned to Your Lesson Plan**

Using the lesson plan you just created, generate assessment questions:

> Based on my lesson plan on "[YOUR TOPIC]", create assessment questions:
> - 5 multiple-choice questions (2 recall, 3 application/analysis)
> - 2 short-answer questions (application level, expected response: 3-4 sentences)
>
> For MCQs: provide the question, four options (A-D), the correct answer, and a 1-sentence explanation of why the correct answer is right.
> For short-answer: provide the question and a model answer.
>
> Ensure questions align with these learning objectives: [PASTE YOUR OBJECTIVES FROM THE LESSON PLAN]

**After generating:**
- Answer each question yourself — is the "correct" answer actually correct?
- Check: are distractors plausible but clearly wrong?
- Check: does each question test a stated objective?

> Speaker notes: 12 min working, 3 min sharing. Stress the verification step — AI-generated MCQs sometimes have multiple correct answers or obviously wrong distractors. More question types and rubric design in **Module 4, Lesson 2**.

## Slide 6: Hands-On — Create a Slide Outline (10 min)

**Activity: Generate a Presentation Outline for Your Lesson**

> Create a 10-12 slide outline for my lesson on "[YOUR TOPIC]" for a [DURATION]-minute lecture.
>
> For each slide provide:
> - Slide number and title
> - 3-4 bullet points (max 8 words per bullet)
> - Talking points (2-3 sentences of what to say)
> - Suggested visual element (diagram, chart, or image description)
>
> Structure: Opening hook (1 slide), key concepts (6-8 slides), summary + discussion (2 slides).

**Key rules for slide prompts:**
- Separate slide text (brief bullets) from talking points (full detail)
- Specify max words per bullet — prevents text walls
- Request visual suggestions even though AI cannot create the graphics

> Speaker notes: Quick activity — 8 min working, 2 min sharing. The goal is a usable slide skeleton they can build into PowerPoint. Full slide prompting strategies in **Module 4, Lesson 3**.

**Module 5: Three high-impact teaching applications**

## Slide 9: Hands-On — Multi-Level Explanations (15 min)

**Activity: Create Three Levels of Explanation for One Difficult Concept**

1. **Choose a concept** your students typically find difficult

2. **Generate three explanations:**

> You are an expert [YOUR FIELD] educator. Explain the concept of "[YOUR CONCEPT]" at three levels:
>
> Level 1 (Introductory): For a student with no background. Use an everyday analogy. No jargon. Max 100 words.
>
> Level 2 (Intermediate): For a student with one introductory course. Use basic terminology with definitions. Include a simple example. Max 150 words.
>
> Level 3 (Advanced): For a final-year student. Full technical vocabulary, nuances, connections to related concepts. Max 200 words.

3. **Evaluate:** Is Level 1 truly accessible to a beginner? Is the analogy accurate? Does Level 3 match your expert understanding?

4. **Bonus:** Generate a locally relevant case study applying the concept:
> Create a short case study (200-300 words) set in a Nigerian business context that requires students to apply the concept of "[YOUR CONCEPT]." Include 3 analysis questions.

> Speaker notes: 12 min working, 3 min sharing. This demonstrates how AI enables differentiated instruction. The "ELI5" technique and more examples are in **Module 5, Lesson 1**. Case study prompting is in **Module 5, Lesson 2**.

## Slide 10: Hands-On — AI-Assisted Student Feedback (15 min)

**Activity: Draft Feedback on a Student Submission**

1. **Choose a type of student work** you regularly assess (essay, report, proposal, problem set)

2. **Use this prompt** (paste a real or simulated student submission):

> You are a supportive but rigorous academic writing coach. Read the following student [WORK TYPE] on "[TOPIC]." Provide feedback structured as:
> - 2 specific strengths (what the student should keep doing)
> - 3 areas for improvement (with concrete revision suggestions)
> - 1 priority action (the single most impactful change)
>
> Tone: encouraging and specific. Avoid vague praise. Every comment should reference a specific part of the submission.
>
> [PASTE STUDENT WORK OR SIMULATED SAMPLE]

3. **Evaluate the AI feedback:**
   - Is it accurate for your field?
   - Would you send this to a student as-is, or does it need your personal touch?
   - What would you add that only you (knowing the student) can provide?

4. **Consider the tiered approach:**
   - Low-stakes work → AI feedback with your quick review
   - High-stakes work → Your personal feedback, AI helps organize

> Speaker notes: 12 min working, 3 min discussion. Emphasize that AI feedback always needs review — it may miss field-specific errors. Ethical considerations (transparency, equity) are covered in **Module 5, Lesson 3** and in Day 2's ethics session.
